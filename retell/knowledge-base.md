## Triage Flows

### Treadmill won't turn on or has no power
*Opener: "Alright, let's figure out what's going on with your treadmill. I'll walk you through a few quick checks — most of the time we can narrow it down pretty fast."*

**Step 1:** Before we start — do you notice any burning smell, see any sparking, or see any burn marks around the power cord or the back of the machine?
  - If Yes: safety_abort
  - If No: proceed to next step

**Step 2:** Okay, first thing — is the safety key in place and pushed all the way in? That's the little clip that usually attaches to you with a lanyard.
  - If Yes: proceed to next step
  - If No: That's probably it. The treadmill won't do anything without the safety key fully seated. Try pressing it in firmly. If it seems damaged or the magnet feels weak, a universal replacement key is about eight dollars on Amazon. Give that a try and let me know if that gets it going.

**Step 3:** Is the power cord plugged directly into a wall outlet — not a power strip or extension cord?
  - If Yes: proceed to next step
  - If No: Go ahead and plug it straight into the wall. Treadmill motors pull a lot of current and they'll trip the surge protector on a power strip without warning. That catches a lot of people. Try that and see what happens.

**Step 4:** Check the back of the treadmill near where the power cord plugs in. Do you see a small reset button or a switch? Some treadmills have a circuit breaker there. If you find one, try pressing the reset button or flipping the switch off and back on.

**Step 5:** Do you see any lights on the console at all — even a dim glow, a flicker, anything?
  - If Yes: proceed to next step
  - If No: With no lights at all, it's likely either a blown internal fuse or the motor controller board. Those aren't really do-it-yourself fixes. Our tech can bring test equipment and usually sort it out in one visit. Want me to get someone scheduled for you?

**Step 6:** So the console has power. When you press start, does the belt move at all — even a little?
  - If Yes: If it starts but struggles or stops quickly, that usually points to worn motor brushes or a slipping drive belt. The good news is brushes are a cheap fix — about fifteen to thirty dollars in parts. Our tech can handle that on-site in under an hour. Want me to get that set up?
  - If No: The console is alive but the belt won't move — that's typically the motor controller board or the wiring between the board and the motor. Our tech will bring the right test equipment to pin it down. Let me grab your info to schedule a visit.

**Needs tech:** This one's going to need a hands-on look from our tech. Let me grab your info so we can get someone out there.
**DIY fix:** Great — glad we got that sorted out. Give us a call back if you run into anything else.
**Safety abort:** Hold on — if you see any sparking, burn marks, or smell anything burning near the power cord or the back of the machine, please unplug it right away and don't plug it back in. That's a potential fire hazard, and we definitely don't want to troubleshoot that over the phone. Let me get a tech out to you as soon as possible.

### Treadmill belt slips, slides to one side, or feels loose
*Opener: "Okay, belt issues are really common and a lot of the time it's a pretty simple fix. Let me walk you through some checks."*

**Step 1:** First, a quick safety question — do you notice any burning smell coming from the treadmill, or does it feel hot underneath the belt?
  - If Yes: safety_abort
  - If No: proceed to next step

**Step 2:** When you say the belt is giving you trouble — is it slipping when you walk or run on it, like your feet push it back? Or is it drifting off to one side?

**Step 3:** When was the last time the belt was lubricated? You can check by lifting the edge of the belt and sliding your hand between the belt and the deck underneath. Does it feel dry, or can you feel some slickness?

**Step 4:** Let's check the tension. With the treadmill off, go to the center of the belt and try to lift one side. Can you pull it up about two to three inches from the deck?

**Step 5:** Does the slipping mainly happen at higher speeds, or when someone heavier steps on it?
  - If Yes: If the belt is lubed and tensioned right but still slips under load, it could be the drive belt — that's the small belt inside the motor cover that connects the motor to the front roller. It can stretch or glaze over time. That one's a bit more involved. Our tech can swap it out on-site, usually takes about forty-five minutes. Want me to set that up?
  - If No: If you're still having trouble after lubrication and tension adjustment, the belt itself might be worn out. Our tech can take a look and bring a replacement belt if needed. Want me to get someone scheduled?

**Needs tech:** Sounds like this one needs a closer look. Let me grab your info and get our tech scheduled to come take a look.
**DIY fix:** Great — that should do it. Keep up with the lubrication every three months or so and you shouldn't have this problem again. Call us back if anything else comes up.
**Safety abort:** Stop right there — if you're getting a burning smell or the deck feels hot under the belt, turn the treadmill off and unplug it right now. That means there's too much friction building up, and running it further could damage the motor or even be a fire risk. We need to get a tech out to check the belt, deck, and motor. Let me get that scheduled for you.

### Burning smell, smoke, or sparking from treadmill
*Opener: "Okay, a burning smell from a treadmill is something we take seriously. Let me ask you a few quick questions so we can figure out what's going on and make sure you're safe."*

**Step 1:** First things first — is the treadmill unplugged right now?
  - If Yes: Good, let's keep it that way until we figure this out.
  - If No: Go ahead and unplug it right now for me. Don't just turn it off — actually pull the plug from the wall. We want it completely disconnected while we talk through this.

**Step 2:** Did you see any actual smoke or sparking, or is it just a smell?

**Step 3:** Can you describe the smell? Does it smell more like hot rubber — kind of like a rubber band burning — or more like an electrical smell, like a burnt circuit board?

**Step 4:** A rubber smell is usually friction-related. When was the last time the belt was lubricated? And has the treadmill been used a lot recently — like heavier use than normal?

**Step 5:** Is the belt very tight? Try lifting the edge of the belt from the center of the deck. Can you get it up about two to three inches?

**Needs tech:** Given the burning smell, I'd recommend having our tech come take a look to be safe. They can check the belt, deck, motor brushes, and controller board all in one visit. Let me get your info.
**DIY fix:** Okay, give that a try — but keep an eye on it. If the smell comes back at all, stop using the treadmill and call us right away. Better safe than sorry.
**Safety abort:** Any time you see smoke or sparks from a treadmill, it stays unplugged until a tech inspects it. Do not plug it back in. This could be a motor, wiring, or controller issue, and it needs to be checked in person. Let me get a tech to you as quickly as we can.

### Elliptical making squeaking, clicking, or grinding noises
*Opener: "Noisy ellipticals are really common, and the fix is usually simpler than you'd think. Let me help you figure out where the sound is coming from."*

**Step 1:** Quick safety check — is it a grinding noise like metal on metal, or more of a squeak or click?

**Step 2:** Let's figure out where the noise is coming from. When you use the elliptical slowly, does the sound happen at the same point in every stride — like at the top or bottom of the pedal motion?

**Step 3:** Check the bolts where the pedal arms connect to the machine — the pivot points where everything moves. Can you see if any of them feel loose? You might be able to jiggle the arm and feel play in it.

**Step 4:** Try this — get some silicone spray lubricant or even WD-40 for now, and spray a little on each of the pivot points where the arms move. Give it a few minutes to work in, then try the machine again. Any change?

**Step 5:** Is the noise coming from down near the pedals or from higher up near the handlebars?

**Needs tech:** Sounds like the bearings or internal components need attention. Let me grab your info and get our tech out there.
**DIY fix:** Excellent — a little lubrication goes a long way. Keep those pivot points sprayed down every month or so and you'll avoid this in the future. Call us if the noise comes back.
**Safety abort:** If you're hearing a loud grinding or feel any sudden catching or locking in the motion, stop using the machine right away. That could be a bearing that's about to seize, and continuing to use it could cause a bigger problem or a safety issue. Let me get a tech scheduled for you.

### Hydraulic fluid leaking from stepper

**Step 3:** How much fluid is leaking? Is it a slow drip, or is there a puddle under the machine?

**Step 4:** The good news is hydraulic cylinders on home steppers are pretty easy to replace yourself. They bolt on with two bolts. The replacement part runs about twenty-five to sixty dollars from the manufacturer. Do you know the brand and model of your stepper?

**Step 5:** Would you like to tackle the replacement yourself, or would you prefer our tech to do it?

**Needs tech:** No problem. Let me grab your info and we'll get someone out there. It's a quick job once we have the right cylinders.
**DIY fix:** You got this. It's a straightforward swap — two bolts per cylinder. Put a towel down to catch any remaining fluid, and clean up any oil on the frame to keep it from getting slippery. Call us if you need any help.
**Safety abort:** If you see fluid leaking onto the floor where you step, clean it up right away — it's a slip hazard. Stop using the machine until the cylinder is replaced. Let me get a tech scheduled if you'd like.

### Stepper steps are uneven — one side harder or different than the other
*Opener: "Uneven steps are annoying, and it's not great for your body either. Let's figure out what's causing it."*

**Step 1:** Before we dig in — do you hear any unusual sounds, like grinding or clunking, when you use the machine?
  - If Yes: What kind of sound is it? Grinding could mean a bearing or bushing issue. Clunking might be something loose. Let me keep going and we'll get to the bottom of it.
  - If No: proceed to next step

**Step 2:** Is this a full-size commercial stepper — the kind with a chain-driven revolving staircase or large independent steps — or a compact home unit with hydraulic cylinders?

**Step 3:** On a home stepper — is one side noticeably softer or easier to push down than the other?
  - If Yes: That's almost always a failing hydraulic cylinder on the soft side. The seal inside is leaking, so it's losing its resistance. ## Common Failure Patterns

### Cable Machine Cable Fraying
- Affects: cable_machine
- Symptom: Visible wire strands poking out of cable, rough feel when pulling cable, cable appears fuzzy or thinning at any point
- Root cause: Fatigue failure from repeated bending cycles around pulleys, sweat corrosion, pulley misalignment causing uneven wear, or age (manufacturers recommend replacement every 2-3 years)
- Typical age: 2 years
- Repair cost: $30-80 cable DIY, $199 flat rate at many shops, $150-300 with labor
- DIY possible: yes

### Cable Machine Weight Stack Selector Pin
- Affects: cable_machine
- Symptom: Pin won't fully engage, weight plate slides off during lift, pin bent or won't slide in, clicking noise from weight stack
- Root cause: Pin bending from being dropped or forced at an angle, guide hole wear, or debris in the pin channel
- Typical age: 3 years
- Repair cost: $5-15 pin replacement, $30-60 with labor
- DIY possible: yes

### Cable Machine Frame Hardware
- Affects: cable_machine
- Symptom: Wobbling or instability during use, clicking from frame joints, visible gap between frame members, bolts found on floor
- Root cause: Hardware loosening from vibration over time, especially on bolted (non-welded) frame connections
- Typical age: 2 years
- Repair cost: $0 (retorque), $5-15 replacement hardware
- DIY possible: yes

### Cable Machine Attachment Carabiner Swivel
- Affects: cable_machine
- Symptom: Carabiner gate won't close fully, swivel seized and won't rotate, attachment disconnects during use
- Root cause: Spring fatigue in carabiner gate, sweat corrosion seizing swivel, or worn latch mechanism
- Typical age: 2 years
- Repair cost: $5-15 carabiner, $10-25 swivel
- DIY possible: yes

### Cable Machine Cable Termination Ferrule
- Affects: cable_machine
- Symptom: Cable pulling out of end fitting, loose ball/stop at cable end, cable end fraying near the ferrule
- Root cause: Crimped ferrule loosening from repeated stress cycles, or corrosion weakening the cable strands at the ferrule junction
- Typical age: 4 years
- Repair cost: $30-80 cable replacement (full cable usually needed)
- DIY possible: no

### Cable Machine Pulley Bearings
- Affects: cable_machine
- Symptom: Grinding or squeaking noise during cable movement, jerky cable motion, cable jumping off pulley track, pulley not spinning freely
- Root cause: Bearing wear from lack of lubrication, dust/dirt contamination, or moisture from sweat and humidity
- Typical age: 3 years
- Repair cost: $10-30 bearings DIY, $80-150 with labor per pulley
- DIY possible: yes

### Cable Machine Cable Routing
- Affects: cable_machine
- Symptom: Cable jumped off pulley, cable rubbing on frame, uneven resistance through range of motion, cable crossed over itself
- Root cause: Cable jumped its track from sudden release of tension (dropping weight stack), worn pulley groove, or improper cable replacement
- Typical age: 2 years
- Repair cost: $0 (re-routing), $30-80 if cable damaged from misrouting
- DIY possible: yes

### Cable Machine Weight Stack Guide Rods
- Affects: cable_machine
- Symptom: Weight stack sticking during lift, grinding noise from weight stack, uneven plate movement, plates tilting
- Root cause: Guide rod corrosion from sweat and humidity, lack of lubrication, bent guide rod from impact, or guide bushing wear in weight plates
- Typical age: 3 years
- Repair cost: $0 (lubricate), $30-60 bushings, $80-200 guide rod replacement with labor
- DIY possible: yes

### Elliptical Stride Arm Bearings
- Affects: elliptical
- Symptom: Squeaking, clicking, or popping sounds during stride, especially at top or bottom of motion. Noise worsens over time
